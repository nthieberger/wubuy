5.4 Further analysis of stem forms

The major formal split in the paradigms (leaving out the Dyadic forms, which are treated separately in §5.6) is between VOC/PROP1 forms and PROP2/PROP3 forms.

This is seen, to "begin with, in suppletion patterns, where entirely distinct roots are used for VOC/PROP1 and PROP2/PROP3 The categories affected by suppletion are 'Fa , 'FaSi', 'Br/Si', 'Mo', 'MoBr', and (classificatory) 'Wi/Hu'. Categories where there is only a faint phonological similarity "between the roots are '♂So/♂Da' and 'FaMo'. PROP2 and PROP3 forms are always based on the same root, with or without minor phonological variations.

For several categories, a prefix /-(r)an -/ is used with PROP2 and PROP3 forms only. The /ng/ is deleted before another nasal by rule P-30. The kin categories with this prefix cluster in grandparental/grandchild generations, and in categories in MoMo's and FaMo's patrilines which are most important to Ego as spouses and affines: 'FaFa', 'MoFa', '♂DaCh', 'MoMo', 'MoMoBrSo', 'MoMoBrDa' (including optional variant C.iv), '♀DaCh', 'Wi', and ’♀MoMoBrDaDa/♂MoFaSiDaSo' . However, in '♀SoCh' (above, this page)

it seems that /-rang-/ is confined to the PROP3 form.

PROP3 is normally distinguished from PROP2 by adding suffix /-yung/, or allomorph /-nyung/ after a nasal. In form, these endings resemble HumSg suffix with non-kin nouns (§4.15) and the Absolute suffix used with demonstratives (§7.7). However, while the PROP2form in some instances has zero corresponding to PROP3 /-yung/ (with 'Fa', 'FaSi', 'Br/Si', 'Mo', 'MoMo', and allowing for regular phonological rules deleting consonants also with ‘♂So/♂Da’, ‘MoFa’, and 'FaMo'), in other instances the PROP2 form ends in a possibly segmentable /j/ ('♂DaCh', 'MoMoBrSo', C.iv form for 'WiMo(Br)', '♀DaCh' , 'Wi', 'Hu', '♀MoMoBrDaDa/♂MoFaSiDaSo') or /yaj/ ('FaMoBrCh'). In 'MoMoBrDa' (C.iii) is looks as though the paradigm has been scrambled: PROP3 /rang-gurumba-yung/ might suggest */rang-gurumbaj/ as a possible PROP2 form by analogy to other paradigms, but in fact we get /rang-gurung/, while the form /gurumbaj/ shows up unexpectedly as the VOC/PROP1 form; Other cases where PROP2 shows some kind of extension involving a consonant are ‘♀So/♀Da’ and '♀SoCh' (but in these two instances the extension is shared with the VOC/PROP1 form).

There are also some minor phonological variations which cannot be accounted for by regular rules, such as differences in final vowel in 'FaFa' (PROP2 /a-muri/ vs. PROP3 /a-muru-yung/, perhaps with a minor assimilation process in the PROP3), medial /b/ vs. /jb/ in '♀MoMoBrDaDa', and /r/ vs. /lh/ in the C.iv variant for 'WiMo(Br)'.

Gender prefixes /niG-/ (once /ni-/) and /ngari-/, for M and F respectively, produce doublets in 'Br' vs. 'Si , '♀So' vs. '♀Da', '♂So' vs. '♂Da', '♀DaSo' vs. '♀DaDa', and '♀SoSo' vs. '♀SoDa'. Usually only a part of the paradigm is affected, but ♂So vs. '♂Da' shows this opposition in all forms. It is possible that initial /ni-/ in /ni-nyara/ 'your Fa' and /ningiyargi/ (/ni-ngiyargi/) 'my/our WiBr' (D.vi) is the same /niG-/, and that /ngari-/ in /ngari-nyina/ 'your FaSi' is /ngari-/.

These gender prefixes resemble the regular M and F NCder prefixes /niG-/ and /ngariG-/ (§4.7, §4.9). However, the NCder prefixes end in /G/, the effect of which is to harden a following continuant to a stop by P-18. In the kin terms, the M form is clearly /niG-/ or is at least consistent with it in most cases, but is /ni-/ in /ni-lhari/ 'your Br' (not */ni-dhari/) and in /ni-wiyay/ 'your (Br)So'. On the other hand, the F form is always /ngari-/ in kin terms, hence such doublets as //niG-w1i//→ /ni-gi/ 'my/our (Br)So' and //ngari-w1i//→ /ngari-yi/ 'my/our (Br)Da' (see also Dyadic forms for 'Br' vs. 'Si', the VOC/PROP1 forms for '♀DaCh', and PROP2 forms for '♀SoCh').

A few remarks are in order concerning the phonology of Pl forms with prefix /mij-/ of PROP2 and PROP3 kin terms. Before /ni(G)-/, /mij-/ shows up as /mi-/ by rule P-26, and before /ng/ it shows up as /miny-/ by P-22. In C.i ('MoMo') we find Dyadic /anggur-yij/ vs. Pl Dyadic /miny-nganggur-yij/, suggesting that this root has two slightly distinct forms, the one with initial /ng/ being used with the Pl prefix.

Similar slight allomorphic variations in roots may help explain other aberrant Pl forms of kin terms. A phonologically regular

Sg/Pl alternation is /ra-ngayang/ 'your MoFa' from //rang-ngayang//, Pl //mij-rang-ngayang//→ //mij-dang-ngayang// P-18→ /mi-da-ngayang/(P-26, P-31). However, the pattern shown by Sg /a-nganggur/ 'your MoMo', Pl /mi-da-nganggur/ is not regular, since *//mij-ang-nganggur// should result in */mi(j)-ga-nganggur/ (involving P-8). It would seem that the PROP2/PROP3 prefix in this paradigm has the short allomorph /ang-/ in the Sg, but the full form //-rang-// in the Pl with preceding /mij-/. It is possible, however, that these Pl kin terms are being reanalysed as involving a prefix allomorph /mid-/ , or else a rule inserting underlying //r// after /mij-/ when the following root or stem begins in /a/; thus note /angamuny/ 'your FaMo', Pl /mi-dangamuny/ and /angiyaj/ 'your FaMoBrCh', Pl /mi-dangiyaj/. In the case of /ngaya-nyij/ 'MoFa and reciprocal' (Dyadic), Pl /mi-da-ngaya-nyij/, it looks as though /-rang-/ has been added between Pl /mij-/ and the root.

Some other alternations associated with /mij-/ can be handled by phonological rules, though admittedly not very productive ones in some instances. In Dyadic forms for 'Fa' (A.ii in ( Table 5-1)), forms with /b/ after /mij-/ suggest an underlying form for the root beginning with //w2//, deleted in other forms by P-12. The same analysis applies to PROP2/PROP3 forms of 'Mo' (B.ii). However, the variants with /g/ in Dyadic forms for 'Fa' can be accounted for either by positing root-initial //w1// which is hardened to /g/ by P-18, or by assuming a root without initial consonant, with /g/ inserted by P-8. This ambiguity is also present with Dyadic form /ali-jij/ 'Hu and Wi' (D.iii in ( Table 5-1)), Pl /mij-gali-jij/, suggesting root //-w1aliG-// or //-aliG-/.

The alternation of Dyadic /rigi-j/ ‘Mo and Ch' with Pl /mi-digi-j/ (B.ii in table) shows //r//→/d/ by Hardening P-18. On the other hand, Dyadic /ramu-nyij/ 'FaMo and reciprocal' (D.i) has Pl /mi-damu-nyij/. Since //r// (tap) should harden to /d/, not /d/ , we assume that /mi-damu-nyij/ involves analogies from cases of /mi-da(ng)-.../ elsewhere in the kin-term system.

The alternation of intervocalic /b/, /jb/, and /nym/ in D.v of the table has no parallels elsewhere and has no apparent phonological basis, except possibly the influence of a following nasal consonant in the (Dyadic) form with /nym/.

Finally, we might mention some partial resemblances among forms listed in the table under different kin categories. First, while 'Mo' and 'MoBr' have a completely different set of forms, there is a similarity between the stem /bibi/ used as VOC/PROPend="subscript">1 form of 'MoBr', and the root /-w2ibi/ used in PROP2/PROP3 forms of 'Mo'. Similarly, while the only actual terminological mergers of categories in 2A and 2D generations are 'FaFa(Si)' = '♂SoCh' (/mu:ri/), there are similarities between 2A and 2D forms in other patrilines also, e.g., /ga:gu/ 'MoMo(Br)' vs. /ga:gula/ '♀DaCh', and possibly /ngayang/ 'MoFa(Si)' and /yalnguya/ '♂DaCh'.
